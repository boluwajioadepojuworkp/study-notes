# From Reading to Notes: The Full Pipeline

Reading and note-writing are often treated as separate activities. You read now. You take notes later. This separation is the source of most reading problems.

The effective alternative treats them as a single integrated pipeline: collect while reading, process after reading, integrate into your permanent notes. Each stage has a specific purpose and a specific method.

## Stage 1: Lightweight Collection While Reading

Do not try to write polished notes while reading. Switching between reading and composing disrupts both. Your brain uses different modes for consuming and creating. Forcing rapid switching exhausts you and produces shallow results in both modes.

Instead, collect observations in the lightest possible way:

- For physical books: tiny notes on a pocket memo pad. One idea per slip. A page number for reference. A dot in the margin at the relevant passage.
- For digital texts: copy and paste key excerpts into a single working document in your writing inbox. Do not organize as you go. Do not format. Just collect.
- For audiobooks or podcasts: voice memo yourself a few keywords that will jog your memory later.

These annotations are not valuable in themselves. They are reminders. They say "look at this passage later" with just enough context to reconstruct your mental state at the time. They are intentionally incomplete. Their job is to be lightweight enough that you actually do them.

## Stage 2: Cluster and Discover Structure

After finishing a chapter or section, look at your collected scraps. Do not try to process them one by one. First, see what patterns emerge.

Spread them out. Group related ones. Notice which ideas keep appearing. Sketch a rough map of how the concepts relate to each other.

The structure you discover does not need to match the text's structure. In fact, it usually should not. The text is organized for the author's purposes. Your notes should be organized for yours. What matters to you may be different from what mattered to the author. The concepts central to your thinking may be peripheral to theirs. That is the point.

## Stage 3: Write Broad Notes, Then Specific Ones

For each cluster of related observations, write a broad note capturing the big idea. What is the core concept? What claim can you make about it? Write the note as if its title is a promise and its body delivers on that promise.

Then write finer-grained notes for the individual observations within that cluster. Each atomic idea gets its own note. Each note makes one clear claim with supporting detail.

Finally, connect everything. Search your existing notes for related concepts. Link the new notes to the old ones. Merge notes that overlap. Revise old notes that need updating based on what you just learned.

This is an iterative process. You may write a broad note, then discover through writing the specific notes that your understanding of the broad concept has changed. Go back and revise. The loop continues until the notes stabilize.

## Stage 4: Let the Literature Notes Fade

Literature notes capture what the author said. They are useful for reference. Keep them around so you can check your sources.

But the permanent value is in your own notes, written in your own words, organized around your own concepts. Once you have extracted the ideas into your note system, the literature notes become secondary. You will rarely need to consult them directly because what matters has already been absorbed and transformed.

## The Physical Detail That Matters

There is one implementation detail that dramatically affects whether this pipeline works: handwritten capture beats typed capture for many people.

Writing on paper while reading a book keeps you in the same physical and mental mode. Typing on a screen creates a hard context switch. Your posture changes. Your attention fragments. You start editing instead of capturing. You get pulled into other apps.

A pocket notebook and a pen solve this. Each idea gets its own tiny page. You process the pages later, one at a time, developing each into a proper note. The physical separation between capture and processing is a feature, not a bug.
