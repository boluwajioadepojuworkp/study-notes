# EAT: The Three Principles of Effective Flashcards

Most people think making good flashcards means using the right card type or installing the right add ons. It does not. Good flashcards come from three principles that must be followed in order.

I call them the **EAT** principles.

## E: Encoded First

You cannot memorize what you have not learned. This is the most important rule and the one most people skip.

Before you make a single card, ask yourself: do I actually understand this? If someone asked me to explain it in my own words right now, could I?

If the answer is no, you are not ready for Anki. Go back to your textbook. Process the material. Make connections. Draw a diagram. Explain it out loud. Only when the idea is IN your head does it make sense to schedule practice.

Making flashcards from unprocessed material is like planting seeds in concrete. Nothing grows.

## A: Atomic

Each card tests exactly one idea. Not two. Not three. One.

Your brain retrieves information through a search process. Vague questions produce vague results. Specific questions produce fast, accurate recall.

Bad: "Describe the mitochondria"
Good: "Where does the Krebs cycle happen?"
Bad: "Explain data communications"
Good: "How is data communications different from other types of communication?"

Atomic cards are fast to answer. Fast cards mean more reviews in less time. More reviews mean stronger memory.

## T: Timeless

You are making cards for your future self. That person does not remember the lecture you just watched. That person does not have the textbook open in front of them.

Your future self needs:
- Enough context to understand the question immediately
- A clear, unambiguous answer
- Optional: a screenshot, a mnemonic, a personal connection

If you look at a card you made last month and think "what was I even asking?", you failed the timeless test.

## The 10 Second Flashcard

When you follow EAT, your flashcards become what I call "10 second flashcards". Here is the structure:

**Front**: A specific question, not a topic label
**Back**: A clear answer plus optional context (image, mnemonic, source, connected idea)

That is it. You do not need fancy card types. A Basic card with a good question does more for your memory than 50 Cloze deletions of random sentences from the textbook.

## Why Making Cards IS Studying

People think making cards is overhead. It is not. When you make a card from a properly encoded idea:

1. You are doing elaborative encoding: finding what to test forces you to identify relationships
2. You are using the generation effect: turning an insight into a question strengthens the memory
3. You are doing retrieval practice: answering your own question before you even review it

Three of the most powerful learning strategies, all happening during card creation. Nothing is more efficient than that.

## Flashcard as Time Assets

A good flashcard is a time asset. You write it once. It saves you time every single day after that. A bad flashcard is a time debt. You write it fast. It costs you time every single day after that.

Plant good seeds.
