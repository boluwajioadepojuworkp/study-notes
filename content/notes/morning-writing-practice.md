The most important part of my day happens before noon. Every morning, for two to three hours, I write and revise notes. This is not "taking notes" in the school sense — copying down what someone else said. This is thinking in writing. Developing my own ideas. Making connections. Sharpening claims.

## Why Morning

Writing is the hardest cognitive work I do. It demands clarity, focus, and the ability to hold multiple ideas in mind simultaneously. By evening, my cognitive resources are depleted. Morning is when my mind is sharpest.

I can sustain this practice for about two to three hours, with five-minute breaks every twenty-five minutes. After that, the quality of my thinking degrades noticeably. I stop. There is no point producing low-quality notes.

## The Ritual

**Step 1: Open the writing inbox.** My inbox contains undeveloped ideas, half-finished notes, one-line prompts, excerpts from my daily log. I flip through them. If something strikes me — a question I want to answer, a claim I want to sharpen, a connection I want to make — I start writing.

**Step 2: Draft an evergreen note.** I write an atomic note with a declarative title. I fill in the idea, link it to existing notes, revise it until it stands on its own. This takes anywhere from five to thirty minutes. Some notes get finished in one session. Others get partially developed and returned to the inbox for another day.

**Step 3: If the inbox is low**, I pull out my pocket memo pad and fill the inbox with new prompts from recent reading and thinking.

**Step 4: Ask generative questions** about my current projects: What are the most important unknowns? What new ideas am I excited about? I use my daily log as scratch space — a dozen one-liners, emptying everything from my head. Then I develop the ones that seem fruitful.

**Step 5: Process the reading backlog.** If nothing else works, I open books I have read but not yet processed, review my annotations, and write evergreen notes from insights that still resonate.

## Signs Something Is Wrong

If none of these steps produce anything for several days, something is broken. My inputs are not varied enough. I am not giving myself the right kind of creative space. The writing inbox should always feel abundant — a cornucopia of prompts waiting to be developed. If it feels empty, I have stopped paying attention to the world.
