An evergreen note is a note designed to live forever. Not to be filed away and forgotten. To be revisited, revised, and connected — constantly, over years, across every project and course I ever work on.

## Why Most Notes Fail

The standard approach fails for one reason: notes are treated as storage. You capture information, organize it by source, and never touch it again. After a year you have a pile of disconnected summaries — Chapter 3 from last semester, Lecture 7 from that one course. None of it adds up to anything.

The problem is not the notes. The problem is the workflow. The work was never about writing notes. The work was always about thinking better.

## What Makes a Note Evergreen

An evergreen note has five properties:

**Atomic.** One note captures exactly one idea. Not one lecture. Not one chapter. One concept. This makes it usable as a building block — you can link to it precisely, revise it without collateral damage, combine it with other atoms to form new thoughts.

**Concept-oriented.** The note is organized around an idea, not around where the idea came from. A note called "Derivatives measure instantaneous rates of change" can link to physics, economics, and biology examples — regardless of which course introduced the concept.

**Densely linked.** Links are not decoration. They are the structure. Every note connects to related notes, prerequisite notes, follow-up notes. The web of connections IS the organization — it replaces folders, categories, and hierarchies.

**Titled with a claim.** The title of an evergreen note is a complete declarative sentence. Not "Photosynthesis." But "Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy through chlorophyll." The title tells you the claim. The body defends it.

**Written for myself.** These notes are not written to teach someone else. They are written so that I can understand the idea six months from now when I have forgotten everything. No background context. No hedging. Raw, honest thinking.

## The Lifecycle of an Evergreen Note

Ideas do not start as evergreen notes. They start as whispers — a sentence you read, a connection you noticed, a question that appeared while walking.

These go into an **inbox**. No structure. No polish. Just capture.

Each morning I process the inbox. Some items get deleted. Some become evergreen notes. The process: write a declarative title, fill in the atomic idea, link it to existing notes. That is it. Five to ten minutes per note.

Then the real work begins: over weeks and months, I pass through old notes while writing new ones. Each pass is an opportunity to revise — sharpen the claim, add a new connection, split a note that grew too large. The note evolves. It gets better every time I touch it.

## Why This Compounds

The dense web of links means that writing a new note forces you to re-read old ones. Connecting "Lagrange multipliers" to "gradient descent" means reopening both notes, re-engaging with both ideas. This is spaced repetition — but organic, driven by the work itself, not by a scheduling algorithm.

After a year, the compound effect is dramatic. When I sit down to write an essay or study for an exam, I do not start from zero. I already have dozens of atomic, linked, revised notes on the topic. Writing becomes editing. The hard thinking was done — one five-minute note at a time, every morning.

## What Does NOT Get a Note

Not everything is evergreen-worthy. Most of what I encounter — news, casual reading, lecture slides I already understand — gets at most a fleeting highlight.

The filter: if I cannot write a complete declarative sentence as the title, the idea is not ready. It stays in the inbox until it crystallizes. Most ideas never do. That is fine.
