# Knowledge Work Should Compound

Most knowledge work is ephemeral. You spend an hour writing a thoughtful email. It lives in your sent folder. You have a deep conversation with a colleague. The insights fade within days. You solve a hard problem. The solution exists only in your head until the next time you face the same problem and have to solve it again.

This is waste. Not because the work was worthless, but because the output was discarded. The thinking did not accumulate.

## The Compound Interest of Thinking

Richard Hamming once asked a colleague how anyone could possibly know as much as John Tukey. The answer: "You would be surprised, Hamming, how much you would know if you worked as hard as he did that many years."

Knowledge and productivity are like compound interest. Two people of equal ability, one working ten percent more, will produce more than twice the output over a lifetime. The more you know, the more you learn. The more you learn, the more you can do. The more you can do, the more opportunities arise.

But compound interest only works if you keep the principal. If you withdraw your savings every month, there is nothing to compound.

In knowledge work, the "principal" is your past thinking. If you do not capture it, develop it, and connect it, you withdraw everything every day. You start from zero each morning.

## Transient Notes Are Not Enough

Most people take notes that serve only the moment. A meeting note that never gets read again. A highlighted passage in a book that fades from memory. A journal entry written in a burst of enthusiasm and never revisited.

These transient notes are like scaffolding. They help you build something in the moment, then get dismantled and discarded. That is fine for some purposes. Sometimes you just need to think through a problem and move on.

But for the ideas that matter repeatedly, transient notes waste the future. Every time you revisit a topic, you should be able to pick up where you left off, not start over.

## What Accretion Looks Like

When your notes are written to endure, each session builds on the last. You open a note you wrote six months ago. It is mostly right. You add a new connection you just discovered. You sharpen an argument that was fuzzy. You remove a claim that turned out to be wrong.

Over years, each note becomes a brick in a structure that grows stronger, not dustier. The structure is not a single document. It is a network of densely linked notes, each about one concept, each with a clear title that makes a claim.

When you read a new book, you do not start a new note for that book. You open the existing notes on the concepts the book discusses and integrate the new perspective. The book's ideas do not sit in a silo. They join a living conversation with everything else you have read and thought.

## The Alternative Is Loss

Without accretion, your thinking is like sand castles at the tide line. You build something elaborate, then the water washes it away. Tomorrow you build again, but you cannot build on yesterday's work because yesterday's work is gone.

With accretion, your thinking compounds. Every insight becomes a foundation for the next. After years of this practice, your notes contain a depth of interconnected understanding that no single session of thinking could produce.

That is the goal. Not to take better notes. To do better thinking, over time, in a way that builds.
