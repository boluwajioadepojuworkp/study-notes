Reading without writing is entertainment. You feel smarter, but nothing sticks. The understanding fades within days.

Writing about what you read is how you actually internalize it. Not highlighting. Not copying quotes. Writing in your own words: making claims, connecting to existing knowledge, extracting the core idea and rebuilding it from scratch.

## Why Highlighting Does Not Work

Highlighting feels productive. It is not. When you drag a yellow line across a sentence, your brain registers the motor activity as "work done" and disengages. You have outsourced the thinking to a color.

Even worse, highlights do not accumulate. A year later, you have fifty books with yellow lines in them. None of it adds up to anything. The insights are trapped inside the books, inaccessible, disconnected from each other.

The alternative: read with a pen (or a notes app open). When something strikes you, capture it briefly. Then, later, write a proper note about it: in your own words, linked to your existing notes.

## The Two-Phase Reading Workflow

### Phase 1: Collect (While Reading)

Do not interrupt your reading flow to write polished notes. That is too heavy. Instead, capture lightweight annotations:

- A sentence that made you stop and think
- A connection you noticed to something you already know
- A question the text raised but did not answer
- A disagreement: "I think this is wrong because..."

Each annotation gets a page number. That is it. No structure, no polish. The goal is to keep reading while capturing enough to reconstruct your thoughts later.

I use a pocket memo pad for physical books and a writing inbox for digital reading. One idea per note. Dozen of tiny scraps.

### Phase 2: Process (After Reading)

Days or weeks later: preferably in the morning, when my mind is clear: I process the pile.

I spread the annotations out. Patterns emerge. Some ideas cluster together. Some were interesting in the moment but seem shallow now: those get discarded. The ones worth keeping become evergreen notes.

For each lasting insight, I write one atomic note:
1. A declarative title making the claim
2. The idea, explained in my own words
3. Links to related notes I already have
4. The book/page reference tucked at the bottom (for my own records)

The key: I do NOT try to summarize the book. I extract the concepts that matter to ME, organized around MY existing knowledge, not around the book's table of contents.

## What Gets a Note and What Does Not

Not every book deserves this treatment. Most of what I read is not worth the hours it takes to write good notes. A news article? No. A casual blog post? Probably not. A textbook chapter I already understand? Just a few quick highlights.

The filter: if I cannot write a sharp declarative title for the idea, it is not ready. If weeks pass and I never feel the urge to write about something I highlighted, it was not important enough.

I write notes for maybe 5% of what I read. That 5% is what compounds.

## Why This Compounds

When I write a note about a concept from one book, and six months later I read another book about the same concept, I do not start from scratch. I open my existing note. I see what I already understood. I compare. The new book might confirm, contradict, extend, or reframe. I update the note.

After a few years, that note has been revised ten times, enriched by five different sources, linked to twenty other notes. It is no longer a "book summary." It is a thinking artifact: denser and more precise than any single source.
