Most people's notes are dead. They write them once, close the notebook, and never look again. A year later they have a graveyard of half-remembered thoughts: fuel that was burned and discarded. That is not how I take notes.

My notes think. They evolve. Every time I open an old note, I revise it. Every time I learn something new, I connect it. Over months and years, my notes compound: each one a brick in a structure that gets stronger, not dustier.

## What Most People Get Wrong

The typical note-taking workflow looks like this: you read something, you underline it, you maybe write a summary in the margin. If you are diligent, you copy quotes into a notebook organized by source. "Chapter 3 notes." "Lecture 5." "Book X highlights."

This is storage. It is not thinking.

Storage notes answer one question: "Where did I put that information?" Thinking notes answer a different question: "What does this mean, and how does it connect to everything else I know?"

Storage notes are organized by where the information came from. Thinking notes are organized by what the information IS: by concept, not by source.

## The Five Principles

After years of experimentation, I have settled on five rules. Every note I write follows them.

### 1. One Note, One Idea

A note should capture exactly one concept. Not a chapter. Not a lecture. One atomic thought.

This sounds restrictive, but it is the opposite. When a note is atomic, it becomes a building block. You can link it anywhere. You can revise it without touching anything else. You can combine it with other atoms to form new ideas.

If a note is about "the chain rule" AND "implicit differentiation" AND "related rates," you cannot link to just the chain rule part. You cannot revise the related rates section without touching everything else. The note becomes a blob: hard to use, hard to maintain.

### 2. Organized by Concept, Not by Source

I do not have a folder called "Calculus Textbook" or "Professor Rossi's Lectures." I have notes called "The derivative measures instantaneous rate of change" and "Limits formalize the idea of approaching."

When you organize by concept, magic happens: you discover that your physics textbook and your calculus textbook are describing the same idea from different angles. The connections reveal themselves because the notes sit next to each other in concept space, even though they came from different courses.

### 3. Dense Linking Is the Structure

The organization of my notes is not a folder hierarchy. It is a web.

Every note links to other notes. Not as an afterthought: as the primary structure. A note about derivatives links to limits, to tangent lines, to velocity, to optimization. Each of those links back. The knowledge graph IS the organization.

This means I never ask "where should I file this?" I ask "what does this connect to?" The answer to the first question is always "it does not matter." The answer to the second question IS the work of thinking.

### 4. Titles Are Claims, Not Topics

A bad note title: "Chain Rule." A good note title: "The chain rule decomposes complex derivatives into simpler ones."

When your titles are complete declarative sentences, they do two things. First, they force you to actually understand what you are claiming: you cannot hide behind a vague topic label. Second, they become API endpoints. You can reference "the chain rule decomposes complex derivatives into simpler ones" from any other note, and the title tells you exactly what you are getting.

### 5. Write for Your Future Self, Not an Audience

When I write notes, I am not trying to be clear for a reader. I am trying to be clear for myself six months from now, when I have forgotten everything.

This means I do not waste time on background context I already know. I do not censor half-formed ideas. I do not add qualifiers and hedges to sound reasonable. The notes are raw thinking: honest, incomplete, evolving.

Publishing them publicly is a separate decision. The thinking comes first.

## The Workflow: From Fleeting Thought to Permanent Note

Ideas do not arrive fully formed. They start as whispers.

I keep an **inbox**: a place to dump thoughts the moment they appear. A sentence I read that struck me. A connection I noticed in the shower. A question I cannot answer yet. No structure, no polish. Just capture.

Once a day: morning, when my mind is clearest: I process the inbox. Some thoughts get discarded. Most get developed: I open a blank note, write a declarative title, fill in the atomic idea, and link it to existing notes.

This morning session is the most important part of my day. It is not "note-taking" in the sense of recording what someone else said. It is thinking in writing: developing my own understanding, making connections, sharpening claims.

## Why This Compounds

Here is the most important insight: **maintaining notes IS spaced repetition.**

Every time I link a new note to an old one, I re-read the old note. Every time I revise a note to incorporate a new insight, I re-engage with the material. The dense web of links forces constant revisiting: naturally, without flashcards, without scheduling algorithms.

After a year of this practice, something remarkable happens. When I sit down to write an essay or prepare for an exam, I do not start from a blank page. I already have fifty atomic notes on the topic. Writing becomes editing: arranging claims into a narrative, filling gaps, connecting threads. The hard thinking was already done, one note at a time.

## What I Do NOT Take Notes On

Not everything deserves an evergreen note. Most of what I read is not worth that investment. A news article, a casual blog post, a textbook section I already understand: these get at most a fleeting highlight.

The notes are for ideas I want to keep thinking about. Ideas that are hard. Ideas that connect to other ideas. Ideas I suspect I will need in a year.

The filter is simple: if I cannot write a declarative title for it: one complete sentence that makes a claim: it is not ready to be a note yet. Back to the inbox it goes.

## The System in Practice

My tools are deliberately simple. Plain text files. Wikilinks with `[[double brackets]]`. A graph view that shows me the web of connections.

The technology does not matter much. You could do this with index cards on a desk. What matters is the practice: every day, one or two atomic notes, each linked to something, each titled with a claim, each revised whenever you pass through it again.

After a year, you have not just a collection of notes. You have a thinking environment: a second brain that actually remembers, actually connects, actually thinks.
