Every day, I start a note titled with the date. It captures everything: reflections, scratch work, half-formed thoughts, things I noticed, things I am confused about, things I want to remember.

This is the lowest-fidelity layer of my system. It is deliberately unstructured. There is no pressure to make it coherent. It is an intentional dumping ground: a release valve so there is always a place to put that thing.

## Why the Daily Log Exists

The daily log solves a simple problem: ideas appear at random times, and if you do not capture them immediately, they disappear. But you cannot stop everything to develop each idea into a proper note. The daily log is the capture zone. Zero friction. A single line is enough.

## The Ladder

The daily log is the bottom of a ladder. Most entries live and die within the day: they served their purpose by getting a thought out of my head. But some entries have legs. A scratch thought crystallizes over days or weeks. At that point, I extract it from the daily log and move it into my writing inbox. From there, it may become an evergreen note.

The ladder: ephemeral scratching → inbox prompt → atomic claim → fully developed idea.

## The Natural Pressure

The daily log is comfortable: easy to dump things into. But it also feels impermanent. This creates a useful pressure. Every few days, I scan recent logs. The entries that still seem interesting get promoted. The rest get archived. The system cleans itself through design, not discipline.

## As Drafting Space

Sometimes I use the daily log as a drafting space. I write a rough version of an idea directly in the log: messy, unstructured, stream-of-consciousness. If it turns out well, I extract it into an evergreen note. The log absorbs the mess. The note captures the result.
