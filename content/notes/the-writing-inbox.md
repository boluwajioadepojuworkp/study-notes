Ideas do not arrive on schedule. They appear in the shower, during a lecture, while walking, in the middle of a conversation. If you do not capture them immediately, they vanish.

But you cannot stop everything to develop each idea into a proper note. You would never get anything done.

The solution is a writing inbox: a frictionless capture zone where thoughts land while they are hot, to be processed later when your mind is clear.

## How the Inbox Works

The inbox is deliberately unstructured. A single line is enough: "Connection between gradient descent and how rivers find the shortest path downhill?" No explanation needed. No context. Just enough to reconstruct the thought later.

I keep one inbox for everything:
- Ideas from reading (page number + one-line thought)
- Questions that appeared while studying
- Connections I noticed between concepts
- Half-formed project ideas
- Interesting phrases or analogies

The inbox is not organized. It is a queue. The only rule: every item must be short enough to write in thirty seconds.

## Why Separation Matters

Mixing transient thoughts with permanent notes is deadly. If your inbox items sit in the same folder as your polished evergreen notes, two things happen:

First, the polished notes feel less polished. They are surrounded by mess. The quality distinction blurs.

Second, and worse, the transient thoughts never get processed. They hide among the permanent notes, invisible, forgotten. The inbox becomes a graveyard of good intentions.

The physical or visual separation: a different folder, a different color, a different app: forces the question: "Have I processed this yet?" Every item in the inbox is implicitly asking for attention. Every item outside it has been handled.

## The Morning Processing Ritual

The inbox only works if you drain it regularly. An inbox that accumulates without processing is worse than no inbox at all: it becomes a source of guilt rather than clarity.

Each morning, I spend twenty to thirty minutes processing the inbox:

1. Open the oldest items first
2. For each: does this still seem interesting?
3. If no: delete it. Most ideas are not worth keeping.
4. If yes: write an evergreen note. Atomic. Titled with a claim. Linked to existing notes.
5. If "yes but not ready": leave it in the inbox for another pass

Some items cycle through the inbox multiple times. An idea might sit for weeks, getting a little more development each morning, until it finally crystallizes into a claim worth writing about. That is fine. The inbox is a nursery, not a factory.

## What I Capture (and What I Do Not)

I capture ideas that have tension: something surprising, confusing, contradictory, or oddly connected. The feeling of "hmm, that is strange" is the most reliable signal.

I do not capture:
- Facts I can easily look up
- Reminders (those go in a task list, not the inbox)
- Things I "should" read later (those go in a separate reading queue)
- Vague feelings without a concrete observation attached

The filter is simple: will I still care about this next week? If I am not sure, it goes in the inbox. The morning processing will answer the question.
