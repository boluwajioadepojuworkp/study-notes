Sharpening Your Notes: Titles, Tools, and Conversations

These are the finer points of a note-writing practice. The principles that separate a good note system from a great one.

Write Titles That Make Claims

A note’s title should be a complete phrase making a sharp claim. Not a topic label like “Memory” or “Note-taking.” But a specific, declarative statement: “Educational objectives often subvert themselves” or “Evergreen notes permit smooth incremental progress.”

This constraint serves multiple purposes. It forces you to identify the core claim of the note. If you cannot summarize your thinking in a single sharp sentence, your thinking is still muddy. The title acts as a litmus test: write the note, then extract the title. If the title emerges naturally, the note is coherent. If you struggle, the note needs more work.

Questions also work as titles when they get to the core of the matter: “To what extent is exceptional ability heritable?” The long-term goal is to eventually drop the question mark by refactoring into a declarative note once you have an answer.

Prefer positive framings. Instead of “Passive environments cannot offer good metacognitive support,” write “Metacognitive supports require dynamic, participatory environments.” Instead of “Do not read on your phone,” write “Use phones to collect and triage, not to read.” Positive titles foreground the theory of why something matters, making it easier to build on in future notes.

Why Digital Tools Are Missing Something

If you write notes on physical index cards, you naturally arrange them on your desk. Related notes cluster together. Foundational notes sit to the left of dependent ones. As you write a new note, you see the others in your peripheral vision. The spatial arrangement IS the sense-making.

Digital note-taking tools fixate on the note you have open. You cannot see the note you just finished writing, let alone the last four. Backlinks help but they show one note at a time. When you navigate among backlinks, you lose object permanence. The spatial relationships that physical cards make natural are absent.

This is not a small problem. It means digital tools optimize for creating notes while ignoring the primary activity: thinking across notes. You end up with a beautiful collection of well-written individual notes and no way to grasp how they relate to each other except by clicking through links one at a time.

Plaintext is the only defense. Use formats that will outlast any app. Use unique identifiers that work across tools. Accept that you are the human connector in a system that has no single application. The thinking happens in your head, guided by the links you created. No software can automate this part.

Notes as Conversation Amplifiers

If a topic you have thought deeply about comes up in conversation, well-developed notes can increase the bandwidth dramatically. Instead of trying to convey complex ideas through the narrow pipe of spoken words, you can share a link to a note that captures your thinking.

Evergreen notes work particularly well here because they are atomic and self-contained. Short enough to be read on the spot. Polished enough to stand on their own. A one-line summary plus a link can evolve a conversation in realtime in a way that referring someone to a book or article cannot.

When multiple people have written notes on related ideas, something even more interesting happens. Instead of figuratively comparing notes, you can literally compare notes. Each person will have factored the ideas differently. Different angles. Different implications. Different contexts. The one-to-one correspondence fails, and that is the whole point. The differences generate new thinking.
